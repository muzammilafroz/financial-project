### 1. The Political Economy of India's Fiscal Federal System and its Reform

- Title: The Political Economy of India's Fiscal Federal System and its Reform
- Authors: M. Govinda Rao, Nirvikar Singh
- Year: 2005
- Journal / Series: Publius: The Journal of Federalism, 35(4), 499–520
- Brief abstract: Analyses the structure of India's fiscal federal system including vertical and
  horizontal imbalances, Finance Commission transfers, and reform directions. Shows that

## Suggested Literature Review Argument for Project 2

The literature should be organised around four claims.

**First**, India's fiscal federal architecture creates a structural vertical imbalance
where most states — and frontier states in particular — rely on central transfers
to close the gap between revenue capacity and expenditure need (Rao and Singh 2005;
Rao 2002; Rangarajan and Srivastava 2011). This is the institutional baseline.

**Second**, fiscal transfer theory predicts that large unconditional transfers reduce
own-revenue effort (Bird and Smart 2002), and Indian panel evidence confirms this
negative relationship between transfer intensity and fiscal discipline
(Bhatt and Scaramozzino 2015). This is the fiscal trap mechanism.

**Third**, FRBM compliance across Indian states is not straightforward to interpret
because deficit definitions vary and because compliance can reflect accounting
treatment of central loans rather than genuine consolidation
(Chakraborty and Dash 2013; Dholakia and Khatua 2016). This supports
the official-vs-broad deficit reconciliation.

**Fourth**, for Northeast frontier states specifically, the transfer architecture
is not merely a developmental tool — it is constitutive of the region's political
relationship with the Indian state, making rapid own-revenue mobilisation
institutionally unlikely in the medium term (Baruah 2020; Oommen 2008).
This elevates the paper from a budget analysis to a structural political economy argument.

The combination of these four claims makes the revenue-surplus paradox in
Arunachal Pradesh a specific instance of a broader pattern that is theoretically
grounded, empirically testable, and institutionally contextualised.
